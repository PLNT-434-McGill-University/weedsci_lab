Ideal Characteristics of a Weed

  • Germination requirements fulfilled in many environments
  • Discontinuous germination and great longevity of seed
  • Rapid growth
  • Continuous seed production & ability to reproduce when young
  • Produce large number of seeds or propagules in favorable environments
  • Self compatible, or out crossing by unspecialized visitors or wind
  • Competitive with desirable plants
  • Genotypic plasticity (the basis of herbicide resistance)
  • Adapted for short and long distance dispersal

Weed Classification

  • Botanical (taxonomic) – based on common traits (i.e. – kingdom, division, class, order, family, genus, species) Originator of scientific nomenclature Carolus Linnaeus, a Swedish botanist, who lived in the 1700’s.

  • Life History – life cycle, season of growth, time and method of reproduction (e.g. – summer/winter annual, biennial, perennial)

  • Habitat – where plant grows (e.g. – terrestrial, aquatic)

  • Physiological – photosynthetic pathway (C3, C4), photoperiod response of flowering (short-day, long-day, day-neutral)

  • Undesirability - difficulty of control (noxious, prohibited), poisonous characteristics

  • Ecological classification – Introduction, colonization, naturalization.

  • Classification by evolutionary strategy – stress tolerators, competitors, ruderals

Common Names

Advantages

  • Well understood within a small locality
  • Easy to pronounce and use by lay people without botanical training
  • Often descriptive (e.g. velvetleaf; Redroot Pigweed)

Common Names

Disadvantages

  • Not accepted everywhere around the world
  • May refer to more than one species
  • Johnsongrass is often used to refer to both Sorghum halepense and Agropyron repens “pigweed” refers to many species of both Amaranthus and Chenopodium.
  • A single plant may have more than one common name
  • Bromus tectorum is often referred to as downybrome, cheat grass, junegrass, and pingrass
  • Abutilon theophrasti is called both velvetleaf and button weed in some US Midwest geographies

Scientific Names

The scientific name always has two parts:

  • The Genus (capitalized)
  • The specific epithet (lower case).

Scientific Names

Advantages

  • only one recognized name per species
  • Provide a means of communication that is accepted around the world: recognized and documented worldwide
  • Stability – they don’t change as often as common names do.

Scientific Names

Disadvantages

  • Some changes do occur as more taxonomic research is conducted.
  • Not descriptive and not widely used by lay people

Taxonomic Classification

  • Monocotyledon (monocots):
    • A class of angiosperms whose seeds have a single cotyledon.
  • Dicotyledon (dicots):
    • A class of angiosperms whose seeds commonly have two cotyledons.

Morphology

Definitions

  • Forb: Herbaceous plants other than grasses and grass-like plants.

  • Shrub: A low-growing woody plant, bush with one to many trunks.

  • Vine: A woody or herbaceous plant that trails on the ground or climbs over other plants or obstacles by various means. The stem of a vine is often so weak that it can not support the plant by itself.

Mallow Family (Malvaceae)

Characteristics

  • Life Cycle: Annual, Perennial
  • Leaves: slimy sap, alternate, usually palmately veined, simple, pubescent.
  • Flowers: Range from small to large, perfect, funnel shaped, 5 separate petals.
  • Fruits: Capsules
  • Cultivated plants of the Mallow Family: Cotton, Okra, Hollyhock (used for making Marshmallow).
  • Class: dicotyledonae

Common Mallow

Also: Cheeseplant, Cheeseweed, Buttonweed

Malva Neglecta

Family: Malvaceae

Common Mallow

Malva neglecta

A winter or summer annual or biennial, with a prostrate growth habit.  Found throughout Canada, more common in turfgrass, landscapes, and nursery crops.

  • Life cycle: Annual or biennial

  • Cotyledons: Heart shaped

  • Stem: Freely branching at the base, lying close to the soil surface, nearly erect or spreading with tips turned up (decumbent).

  • Leaf: Alternate arrangement, on long petioles, circular to kidney-shaped, toothed and shallowly 5-9 lobed.

Common Mallow

Malva neglecta

  • Flowers: Single or in clusters of 2-4 in leaf axils. Petals white or tinged with pink or purple.

  • Fruit: Flattened, round, disc-like, composed of 12-15 small hairy, 1-seeded segments, 5 to 8 mm in diameter, resembling a button in appearance

  • Identifying Characteristics: Fruit disc-like, resembling a button. Leaves circular, toothed, and long petioled. This weed is often confused with Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea).  However, ground ivy leaves are opposite, and have much more prominent rounded teeth.  Ground ivy also has square stems and may emit a minty odor.

Field bindweed

Convolvulus arvensis

  • Life cycle: Perennial

  • Cotyledons: slightly butterfly shaped lobes

  • Leaves: Simple

    • Long-petioled attachment
    • Alternate arrangement
    • Arrowhead shape with somewhat rounded tip
    • Smooth surface

Field bindweed

Convolvulus arvensis

  • Stem: twining, without hair to pubescent

  • Roots: Extensive roots (up to 10m), rhizomes

  • Found: small grain fields, gardens, roadsides

  • May cause mild distress in swine

  • One of the most serious weeds in Nebraska

  • Thought to have been introduced through wheat seed from Turkey in 1870
